Why ISAT

What the major is

Integrated Science and Technology (ISAT) is James Madison University's B.S. program built around solving real-world problems rather than staying inside one traditional science discipline. Instead of specializing narrowly from day one, ISAT students build a broad foundation across the basic sciences alongside technology, computing, and problem-solving skills, then apply all of it together to practical, often messy, real-world challenges.

A defining piece of the curriculum is its "holistic problem-solving" course sequence, developed over all four years, which trains students to think in systems — technical, institutional, cultural, and natural — rather than relying on the narrower toolkit of a single discipline. The program is ABET-accredited in applied science and lets students tailor a concentration (in my case, Manufacturing) to fit their own interests.

Why I chose it

When I started at JMU, I hadn't settled on one discipline yet. ISAT appealed to me because it let me explore a few different majors before committing to a concentration in my junior year, rather than locking into a single path from day one. It's also a noticeably more hands-on program than most, and it sits within the College of Integrated Science and Engineering — squarely in the space I want to build a career in.

Skills it's given me

JMU's ISAT program builds a broad foundation of scientific and technical skills, then lets you customize that foundation through a concentration — mine being Manufacturing, alongside options like biotechnology, applied computing, energy, and environment for other students in the program. The core of it is multidisciplinary problem-solving: connecting complex technical concepts across disciplines rather than staying siloed in one. Along with that, it's built up my data analysis and project management skills, and my ability to think through how a technical decision connects to broader societal and ethical considerations, not just the engineering itself.
